## Wide swings versus tight peeks versus jiggle peeks
A wide swing exposes you to multiple angles at once by peeking far off the wall —
high risk, only justified when you have a trade behind you or need to clear a known
angle fast. A tight peek hugs the wall so you reveal one angle at a time and can
retreat behind cover — the default for gathering info or taking a duel you control.
A jiggle peek steps out and back to bait a shot or read a hold without committing.
If your deaths cluster on wide swings, you are over-exposing: default to tight
peeks and only swing wide with support.

## Fixing a wide-swing habit
Frequent wide-swing deaths mean you commit your whole body into space before you
know what is there. Replace the habit: pre-aim the angle at head height, peek with
your crosshair already on the likely head position, and take one angle at a time so
you can duel or retreat instead of getting caught by a crossfire. Peek with a
plan — know your escape and whether a teammate can trade you before you step out.

## Crossfire deaths and trade discipline
Dying repeatedly in crossfires means you are entering space covered by two or more
angles alone. The fix is trade spacing: enter within 3–5 meters of a teammate so
they can punish whoever kills you, clear the close corner first, and never be the
second player to peek the same angle that just traded a teammate. On defense, set
up your own crossfires with a teammate rather than holding wide solo.

## Crosshair discipline while repositioning
The most common positioning leak is dropping the crosshair to the floor while
rotating or repositioning during downtime. Hold head height at all times so any
sudden contact is a short adjustment, not a full re-aim from the ground. Pre-aim
common angles as you move through the map, and re-settle the crosshair to
head-height cover after every rotation.

## Anchoring, delay, and the retake
When you lose a site or a fight, do not trade yourself into a lost round. As a
defender your job is to delay the execute 10–15 seconds with utility and off-angle
picks so teammates rotate, then retake together — not to hold a site 1v5. In a
clutch, isolate duels: use cover to turn a 1vX into separate 1v1s, play the clock,
and take fights on your terms rather than challenging multiple angles at once.
